Three-Month Typing & Computer Skills Training Plan

Created by Dr. Arnab Chaudhuri

This is a three‑month course with 5 sessions per week (about 60 sessions in total). Each session can be 10–20 minutes, kept light and enjoyable.

Overall structure

  • Month 1: Readiness, pre‑computer skills, and random letter/number typing.
  • Month 2: Name, favourite words, CVC words, and spacebar/Enter.
  • Month 3: Sentences, paragraphs, basic formatting, mouse use, and functional practice.

Month 1 (Weeks 1–4): Readiness and Letter Typing

Week 1 (Days 1–5): Prerequisites & Pre‑Computer Skills

  1. Day 1: Practise comfortable sitting at a table for 5–10 minutes, with a dummy or printed keyboard, allowing exploration and touching keys.
  2. Day 2: Work on following 1–2 step instructions like “Touch A”, “Find 3” using the dummy keyboard, with clear verbal and visual prompts.
  3. Day 3: Play alphabet identification games away from the computer (flashcards, alphabet chart) focusing on capital letters A–Z.
  4. Day 4: Play number identification games (0–9); reinforce up/down and right/left through simple body movements and pointing games.
  5. Day 5: Match letter and number cards to positions on the dummy keyboard, building hand–eye coordination and random tracking of symbols.

Week 2 (Days 6–10): Transition to Real Keyboard

  1. Day 6: Introduce the real keyboard; let the child explore keypresses with supervision, emphasising pressing one key at a time.
  2. Day 7: Choose 3–5 easy letters (e.g., A, B, C, D, E); show flashcard → child finds and presses that letter; use praise or tokens.
  3. Day 8: Add 2 more letters; practise all 5–7 letters in random order, keeping the pace slow and non‑stressful.
  4. Day 9: Turn it into a “typing game”: you say a letter, the child finds and presses it; end after a few successful trials.
  5. Day 10: Review the same set of letters, noticing which letters need extra practice, and repeat in playful cycles.

Week 3 (Days 11–15): Building Random Alphabet Typing

  1. Day 11: Expand to 8–10 letters; practise typing one letter at a time in random order, showing the letter on a card or screen.
  2. Day 12: Open a simple blank document so the child can see letters appearing on screen when they type.
  3. Day 13: Mix all known letters; ask for 5–10 letters per session, focusing on accuracy and calm behaviour rather than speed.
  4. Day 14: If ready, introduce a few lower‑case versions of known letters; otherwise continue with capitals only.
  5. Day 15: Briefly show upper vs lower case pairs (A/a, B/b) using large print or cards, connecting them to the same key on the keyboard.

Week 4 (Days 16–20): Consolidation and Numbers

  1. Day 16: Review all known letters with a “letter hunt” game on the keyboard (e.g., “Find H”, “Find S”).
  2. Day 17: Introduce 0–4 number keys; ask the child to type simple sequences such as 1‑2‑3.
  3. Day 18: Introduce 5–9 number keys; practise identifying and typing them in order and randomly.
  4. Day 19: Alternate short sets of letter typing and number typing within one session to promote flexibility.
  5. Day 20: Combine: type short random sequences with both letters and numbers (e.g., A–3–B–2), ending with an easy, successful trial.

Month 2 (Weeks 5–8): Names, Favourite Words & Special Keys

Week 5 (Days 21–25): Typing the Child’s Name

  1. Day 21: Show the child’s name in large print; help them locate each letter on the keyboard with hand‑over‑hand or pointing prompts.
  2. Day 22: Repeat name typing with slightly reduced physical prompts, shifting to verbal cues (“Find N”) and visual highlighting.
  3. Day 23: Ask the child to type their name 2–3 times, giving praise or preferred items after each correct attempt.
  4. Day 24: Increase to 3–5 repetitions of name typing, gently correcting any missed letters and celebrating effort.
  5. Day 25: Mix 1–2 random letters plus name typing; always end the session with a successful name attempt to boost confidence.

Week 6 (Days 26–30): Favourite Words

  1. Day 26: Choose 2–3 favourite words (e.g., MOM, DAD, BALL, CAR, favourite character’s name) and show them with clear print and pictures.
  2. Day 27: Help the child type each favourite word once, then show the related picture or object as immediate reward.
  3. Day 28: Encourage more independent attempts at favourite words by waiting longer before prompting.
  4. Day 29: Mix name and favourite words in one session (NAME → MOM → NAME → BALL), giving frequent breaks and positive feedback.
  5. Day 30: Create a small “My words” document where the child types their name and favourite words as a word list.

Week 7 (Days 31–35): CVC and Short Words

  1. Day 31: Introduce very short CVC or high‑frequency words like IN, IS, UP, CAT, DOG, MAT, using picture cards when available.
  2. Day 32: Practise typing 2–3 different CVC words with your modelling, then let the child try independently.
  3. Day 33: Start simple “typing dictation”: you say “cat”, child types CAT while looking at a picture; correct gently and encourage retries.
  4. Day 34: Add a few new CVC words while reviewing the earlier ones in a small mixed list.
  5. Day 35: Expand the “word bank” document by adding all learned CVC words in a neat list.

Week 8 (Days 36–40): Introducing Spacebar and Enter

  1. Day 36: Mark the spacebar and Enter keys with small stickers; show matching cards and let the child match card → key.
  2. Day 37: Practise typing one short word, then pressing spacebar, using modelling and verbal prompts.
  3. Day 38: Continue word + spacebar practice, gradually fading prompts as the child anticipates the space after each word.
  4. Day 39: Practise typing a word, then pressing Enter to move to a new line; show visually what happens on screen.
  5. Day 40: Combine: type a short horizontal sequence of words with spaces and use Enter at the end of the line.

Month 3 (Weeks 9–12): Sentences, Paragraphs & Functional Skills

Week 9 (Days 41–45): Simple Sentence Typing

  1. Day 41: Introduce very short sentences with strong visual support, e.g., “I see a cat.” with a cat picture; child copies from a card.
  2. Day 42: Practise 1–2 line sentences, focusing on spaces between words and using a period at the end.
  3. Day 43: Repeat sentence copying with different simple sentences about familiar objects or people.
  4. Day 44: Slightly reduce visual support by covering part of the sentence while the child types, prompting only when needed.
  5. Day 45: Let the child choose one sentence about something they like (e.g., “I like cars.”) to type 2–3 times.

Week 10 (Days 46–50): Building Short Paragraphs

  1. Day 46: Group 2 familiar sentences into a small paragraph about a known topic (e.g., “I like mango. Mango is sweet.”) and have the child copy it.
  2. Day 47: Practise another short paragraph on a different topic, still keeping each sentence short and clear.
  3. Day 48: Begin an “about me” paragraph with the child (name, age, favourite food/toy), typing together.
  4. Day 49: Ask the child to retype the “about me” paragraph with reduced help, prompting when stuck.
  5. Day 50: Revisit one of the earlier paragraphs to consolidate learning and improve fluency.

Week 11 (Days 51–55): Early Formatting and Mouse Use

  1. Day 51: Show how to use the mouse or touchpad to move the pointer and click; practise simple click games or desktop icons.
  2. Day 52: Practise single‑click and double‑click actions in a safe environment (e.g., opening a simple painting or typing program).
  3. Day 53: Introduce basic text selection (click and drag over a word) and one formatting option like making text bold or changing font size.
  4. Day 54: Practise opening and closing a document file with support, typing a short sentence, and saving it.
  5. Day 55: Combine typing a short paragraph with one basic formatting change (e.g., making the title bold or slightly larger).

Week 12 (Days 56–60): Functional Practice & Generalisation

  1. Day 56: Create a simple daily routine list (e.g., “Wake up”, “Brush teeth”) that the child types and decorates with basic formatting.
  2. Day 57: Practise typing short messages (e.g., “Hello Grandma”) in a safe, supervised space like a draft email or note.
  3. Day 58: Let the child choose a topic (favourite animal, cartoon, food) and type a 3–4 sentence “mini‑project” paragraph with scaffolding.
  4. Day 59: Review the three‑month journey: retype name, favourite words, and one short paragraph, noticing how much easier it feels now.
  5. Day 60: Combine skills in a celebration session: type name, one favourite word, one sentence, and one short paragraph, apply a simple formatting change, and praise the child with family present.

Caregiver tips

  • Keep sessions predictable: same time, same place, and a short, clear routine (greet, practise, praise, close).
  • Use high‑interest words and topics so that typing feels meaningful, not just mechanical drill.
  • Watch for signs of overload (restlessness, frustration, withdrawal) and stop early rather than forcing completion.
  • Frequently celebrate small achievements with verbal praise, tokens, or preferred activities, supporting motivation and self‑confidence.
